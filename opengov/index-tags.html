Rebuilding public trust in open data

OpenGov Transparency

Brand Identity
Art Direction
User Experience Design
Data Visualization

Visual Design
Design System
Motion Design
Iconography

Over the last decade, a growing number of government organizations have begun to make budgetary and financial data available online. Governments began to ask themselves, "How can we make our finances more transparent?" since merely making those difficult-to-understand 500-page budget sheet PDFs available for download isn't enough.

As the first employee at OpenGov, I was tasked with tackling this difficult challenge. Early prototypes were developed to assess market demand for a better way to display the government's massive financial data. As we gained more consumers, we received a stream of feedback, which assisted us in identifying several major issues. We evaluated the feature requests, bug tickets, and market reports, key issues and themes began to emerge, such as confusing navigation and filter tools that are difficult to discover. We ran several usability tests remotely, confirming the same core issues.

One of the most important findings is that the aesthetic is misleading. When we asked people to describe their impressions of the product, we often heard remarks about how sleek it felt, which contradicts the product's promises of evoking sentiments of transparency. As a result, we decided to make a big change. And after months of user research and usability testing, the ideas began to flow. Slowing down long enough to capture each new idea proved our greatest challenge.
